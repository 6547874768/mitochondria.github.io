Enzyme Dental Care Research: Systematic Review Shows Mutanase Superior to Dextranase

2024 systematic review of 34 studies: Mutanase more effective than dextranase for biofilm control. Lactoperoxidase system clinical trials show bacterial composition changes. Combined enzyme approach documented.

Research Summary: This article reviews published enzyme dental research. Information is educational and based on scientific studies. Consult dental professionals for personalized recommendations.

"2024 Systematic Review Analyzes 809 Research Records"

Published in Biofilm Research journal, May 2024, researchers from Aarhus University conducted the most comprehensive enzyme review to date. The team screened 809 records, ultimately analyzing 34 high-quality studies on dental enzymes.

The review examined three categories: dextranase (23 studies), mutanase (10 studies), and combined treatments (7 studies). Most investigations used treatment times of 30 minutes or longer.

Key finding: Mutanase demonstrated stronger effects on biofilm formation compared to dextranase. Enzymes proved more effective for biofilm prevention than removal of established biofilms.

The evidence suggests these matrix-degrading enzymes offer non-biocidal alternatives to traditional antimicrobials, potentially avoiding resistance development. Results vary based on enzyme concentration and application method.

Mutanase vs Dextranase: What 34 Studies Reveal

The systematic review provides clear hierarchy of effectiveness. Among 809 screened records, researchers identified consistent patterns. Mutanase targets α-1,3-glucosidic bonds - the primary structural component of water-insoluble glucan. Dextranase breaks α-1,6-glucosidic bonds found in water-soluble portions.

The structural difference matters. Biofilm Research 2024 documented that mutanase alone can inhibit and remove cariogenic biofilms. Dextranase requires longer treatment times and shows lower overall effectiveness. Combined treatment didn't significantly improve outcomes beyond mutanase alone in most studies.

Treatment duration proved critical. Studies employing less than 30 minutes showed minimal effects. Those using 60+ minute treatments achieved measurable biofilm reduction. This suggests practical application challenges for daily use.

Lactoperoxidase System: Clinical Evidence from Multiple Trials

The lactoperoxidase (LPO) system represents a different enzymatic approach. International Journal of Molecular Sciences (2019) reviewed clinical trials of this natural defense system. LPO oxidizes thiocyanate ions using hydrogen peroxide, producing hypothiocyanite with antimicrobial properties.

BMC Microbiology 2021 crossover study tested LPO lozenges. The four-replicate design with 16 participants showed the system didn't reduce total bacterial count but shifted composition. Cariogenic bacteria decreased while commensal flora remained stable - exactly the selective pressure desired.

Scientific Reports 2017 documented microbiome shifts. Adams and colleagues tested enzyme toothpaste containing LPO system on plaque ecology. Results: 12 taxa associated with health increased (including Neisseria species), while 10 taxa linked to periodontal disease decreased (including Treponema species).

Practical application exists. Multiple studies show LPO-containing products maintain activity in toothpaste formulations. The enzyme transfers to pellicle where it remains catalytically active. Products like natural gum supplements may support this system's function.

🔬 Chimeric Enzymes: Japanese Innovation

Research Update: Tsurumi University created fusion enzymes combining mutanase and dextranase properties.

Microbiology and Immunology 2015 published breakthrough research. Japanese scientists at Tsurumi University created chimeric glucanase - a fusion of mutanase and dextranase. This bi-functional enzyme showed 4.1 times greater effectiveness than enzyme mixtures against biofilm formation.

The mechanism involves collision frequency. One chimeric molecule possesses two functional domains. This increases substrate interaction compared to separate enzymes that must find targets independently. The research team confirmed both prevention of biofilm formation and decomposition of existing biofilm.

Interface Oral Health Science 2012 confirmed synergy. The combination of mutanase and dextranase effectively suppressed formation of insoluble glucan biofilm by Streptococcus mutans and S. sobrinus. The enzymes convert adherent water-insoluble glucan to non-adherent forms.

Commercial application remains limited. Despite promising research, chimeric enzymes haven't reached market. Production complexity and stability issues likely contribute. Current products like herbal alternatives use different mechanisms while research continues.

Dextranase Research: Food-Grade Applications

PMC published research on dextranase encapsulation in alginate beads for toothpaste application (2020). The study addressed enzyme stability challenges - a major barrier to commercial use. Optimal encapsulation occurred at pH 7 with 20% calcium chloride and 0.85% sodium alginate.

Activity measurements showed promise. Food-grade fungal dextranase achieved 4401.71 units/g at pH 6 and 37°C. The enzyme at 4.5 units/g (minimum inhibitory concentration) strongly inhibited S. mutans growth. Half this concentration still reduced biofilm formation significantly.

Stability improved with encapsulation. Toothpaste containing encapsulated dextranase remained stable after 3 months storage. Sensory testing showed acceptance at "like slightly" level. This addresses the major commercial challenge - maintaining enzyme activity in consumer products.

However, the systematic review context matters. While dextranase shows activity, the 2024 review confirmed mutanase superiority. Dextranase targets water-soluble glucans which form smaller portion of biofilm matrix. This explains lower overall effectiveness despite measurable activity.

Research-Based Answers About Enzyme Dental Care

Which enzyme is most effective for dental biofilm removal?
According to the 2024 systematic review of 34 studies published in Biofilm Research, mutanase showed overall higher effectiveness compared to dextranase for both biofilm inhibition and removal. The review found enzymes more effective for preventing biofilm formation than removing established biofilms. Mutanase targets α-1,3-glucosidic bonds in water-insoluble glucan, the primary structural component.
How does the lactoperoxidase system work in oral health?
Lactoperoxidase oxidizes thiocyanate ions in the presence of hydrogen peroxide to produce hypothiocyanite, which has antimicrobial properties. BMC Microbiology 2021 and Scientific Reports 2017 show this system doesn't reduce total bacterial count but shifts composition - decreasing pathogenic species while maintaining beneficial bacteria. The system requires all three components to function.
What contact time do enzymes need to be effective?
The systematic review found most successful studies employed treatment times of 30 minutes or longer. Studies using less than 30 minutes showed minimal effects. This presents practical challenges for daily application, as most people brush for 2-3 minutes. This may explain why enzyme toothpastes show variable results in real-world use.
Can enzymes replace traditional oral hygiene?
No. Research positions enzymes as adjuncts to mechanical plaque control, not replacements. The 2024 review specifically notes enzymes as "non-biocidal adjuncts" to traditional methods. They may reduce reliance on antimicrobials that risk resistance development, but mechanical removal remains essential.
Why aren't enzyme products more common?
Multiple factors limit commercialization: enzyme stability in formulations, required contact times (30+ minutes), production costs, and regulatory requirements. The 2020 dextranase encapsulation study addressed stability, but cost and contact time remain challenges. Most current products use simpler, more stable ingredients.

Practical Insights from Enzyme Research

🎯 Contact Time Reality

Research shows 30+ minutes needed for enzyme effectiveness, yet most people brush 2-3 minutes. This gap explains variable real-world results. Consider enzyme rinses or gels that can remain in mouth longer. Some studies used enzyme-containing gum for extended contact. Overnight applications in custom trays showed promise in research settings.

⏰ Prevention vs Treatment

Systematic review clearly shows enzymes prevent biofilm better than removing established films. This suggests using enzyme products after professional cleaning when biofilm is minimal. Morning application may prevent daytime accumulation. Focus on maintaining clean state rather than treating heavy buildup.

🧪 pH and Temperature

Dextranase shows optimal activity at pH 6 and 37°C (body temperature). Lactoperoxidase system requires specific pH range. Acidic conditions after eating may reduce effectiveness. Consider timing enzyme use when mouth pH is neutral. Room temperature storage maintains stability according to encapsulation studies.

💰 Cost-Benefit Analysis

Enzyme products typically cost more than standard toothpastes. Research shows selective bacterial reduction rather than total elimination. This may justify cost for those seeking to avoid antimicrobial resistance. Consider targeted use during high-risk periods rather than continuous application.

Trichoderma Harzianum: Fungal Source of Mutanase

Research published in International Journal of Biological Macromolecules (2015) explored alternative mutanase sources. Trichoderma harzianum, a fungus, produces mutanase when grown on specific substrates. Laetiporus sulphureus (a polypore fungus) cell walls proved superior to traditional mutan for enzyme induction.

Production improved significantly. T. harzianum F-340 grown on L. sulphureus cell walls achieved 0.71 U/ml mutanase activity after 3 days. This was 1.8-fold higher than using expensive, inaccessible mutan substrate. The fungal cell walls contained 56.3% α-1,3-linked glucans, explaining their effectiveness.

The enzyme showed practical application. Cell-wall-induced mutanase achieved 80% saccharification and 100% solubilization of dextranase-pretreated mutan in 3 hours at 45°C. It effectively degraded streptococcal mutan and removed it from oral biofilms, especially combined with dextranase.

This addresses production bottlenecks. Traditional mutanase production requires mutan from bacterial cultures - expensive and limited. Using fungal substrates could reduce costs and increase availability. However, food-grade approval and scaling remain challenges before commercial application.

⚠️ Research Limitations and Considerations

Important factors from published studies:

  • Most research used simplified biofilm models, not complex oral environments
  • 30+ minute contact times impractical for daily brushing

  • Individual oral microbiomes vary significantly
  • No long-term safety data for continuous enzyme use
  • Enzymes complement but don't replace mechanical cleaning
  • Cost-effectiveness compared to standard care unproven
  • Regulatory approval varies by country
  • Results from in vitro studies may not translate to clinical use
