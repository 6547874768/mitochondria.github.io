European Food Safety Authority evaluated L.reuteri DSM 17938 + ATCC PTA 5289 for "normal gum function" claim. BMC Microbiology 2021: Dose-Dependent Colonization

75 subjects received four different doses of M18 for 28 days. About 60% already had natural bacteriocin-producing S.salivarius. Persistence appeared to depend on dose, not duration. Some subjects showed replacement of their native strains. DNA sequencing suggested total S.salivarius didn't increase - introduced strain may have displaced natives. Gene transfer between strains was observed, potentially creating enhanced antimicrobial producers.

Molecular Questions, Evidence-Based Answers

How does L.reuteri produce reuterin and why does dose matter?
L.reuteri converts glycerol to reuterin (an antimicrobial compound) using a vitamin B12-dependent enzyme. The reaction happens in specialized compartments (like tiny factories inside the bacteria) to protect the cell. At 2√ó10‚Å∏ CFU, each bacterium may have better glycerol access. At 10¬π‚Å∞ CFU (common in commercial products), competition for nutrients might reduce per-cell production. Bacterial communication at high density may also affect production.
Why doesn't S.salivarius K12 work for everyone?
About 60% of people already have bacteriocin-producing S.salivarius naturally. K12 needs to compete with these existing strains for space in the mouth. Success may depend on your current oral bacteria. Strain K12 produces specific antimicrobials (salivaricin A2 and B), but if your native strains produce similar compounds, you might not see additional benefit. The genetic stability also varies - some individuals may lose bacteriocin production after 14 days.
What's the difference between DSM 17938 and generic L.reuteri?
DSM 17938 is a specific genetic variant stored in the German Collection of Microorganisms. It's been genetically sequenced, tested for reuterin production, and studied in clinical trials. Generic "L.reuteri" could be any of hundreds of different strains, most producing little or no reuterin. Some might even produce histamine (potentially causing inflammation). The strain designation helps ensure you get the studied organism, not a random relative.
Can probiotics replace professional dental care?
No. Established biofilms with calcified tartar require mechanical removal. Probiotics may help maintain healthy balance after professional cleaning. Studies showing benefit combined probiotics with scaling/root planing. Think of probiotics as maintenance between professional visits, not replacement. They may help reduce harmful bacterial load but can't reverse existing pocket formation or bone loss.
